Near-Field Communication (NFC)

A short-range wireless communication technology that enables data exchange between devices over a distance of a few centimeters.

Description

Near-Field Communication (NFC) is a set of communication protocols that enables two electronic devices to establish communication by bringing them within close proximity, typically no more than 4 centimeters apart. NFC operates at 13.56 MHz and can transfer data at rates up to 424 kbit/s. It is commonly used for contactless payment systems, allowing users to make transactions simply by tapping their mobile devices against a compatible point-of-sale terminal. Beyond payments, NFC is also utilized in applications such as ticketing, access control, and data sharing between devices, like transferring files or connecting to Bluetooth devices. NFC technology is based on the established RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification) standards, but it allows for two-way communication, making it versatile for various use cases. With the rise of smartphones equipped with NFC capabilities, its adoption has grown, enhancing user convenience and security in digital transactions.

Examples

  • Contactless payment systems like Apple Pay and Google Wallet.
  • NFC tags used for marketing that can be scanned by smartphones.

Additional Information

  • NFC is secure due to its short range, which limits eavesdropping.
  • NFC devices can operate in three modes: reader mode, peer-to-peer mode, and card emulation mode.
