Sarbanes–Oxley Act (SOX)

A U.S. federal law enacted to protect investors from fraudulent financial reporting by corporations.

Description

The Sarbanes–Oxley Act (SOX), enacted in 2002 in response to major corporate and accounting scandals, aims to enhance corporate governance and accountability. It was designed to improve the accuracy and reliability of corporate disclosures and to protect investors from fraudulent accounting practices. SOX imposes strict reforms to improve financial disclosures from corporations and establishes a Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to oversee the audits of public companies. Key provisions of SOX include the requirement for senior executives to personally certify the accuracy of financial information, the establishment of internal controls and procedures for financial reporting, and the requirement for external auditors to report on the effectiveness of these controls. Companies are also required to maintain records for a minimum of seven years. The act has had a significant impact on how corporations report their financial health and has contributed to higher standards of financial accountability and transparency in the corporate sector.

Examples

  • Public companies must comply with SOX regulations to ensure the integrity of their financial reports.
  • An example of SOX's impact is the requirement for companies to implement internal controls over financial reporting.

Additional Information

  • SOX applies to all publicly traded companies in the U.S. and their wholly-owned subsidiaries.
  • Violations of SOX can lead to severe penalties, including fines and imprisonment for corporate executives.
