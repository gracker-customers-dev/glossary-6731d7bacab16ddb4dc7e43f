Blockchain

A decentralized digital ledger technology that records transactions across multiple computers.

Description

Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology that enables secure and transparent record-keeping without the need for a central authority. Each block in the blockchain contains a list of transactions, and once a block is filled, it is cryptographically linked to the preceding block, forming a chain. This structure ensures that any attempts to alter the data in one block would require changes to all subsequent blocks, which is computationally impractical. As a result, blockchain provides a high level of security and trust, making it suitable for various applications beyond cryptocurrencies, such as supply chain management, healthcare, and identity verification. In the context of Identity and Access Management (IAM), blockchain can enhance security by enabling self-sovereign identity models, where individuals control their own identity data and share it selectively with services as needed. This approach can reduce reliance on centralized identity providers and minimize the risk of data breaches.

Examples

  • Bitcoin, a decentralized cryptocurrency that uses blockchain to record transactions.
  • Ethereum, a platform that enables smart contracts and decentralized applications on its blockchain.

Additional Information

  • Blockchain can be public (permissionless) or private (permissioned), depending on access control requirements.
  • It employs consensus mechanisms such as Proof of Work or Proof of Stake to validate transactions and maintain integrity.
