Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

A U.S. legislation that provides data privacy and security provisions to safeguard medical information.

Description

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) was enacted in 1996 to improve the efficiency of the healthcare system and protect patient privacy. HIPAA sets national standards for the protection of health information, ensuring that individuals' medical records and personal health information are properly secured. The legislation includes provisions that allow patients greater control over their health information while imposing strict rules on healthcare providers, health plans, and business associates regarding the handling of this data. HIPAA's Privacy Rule establishes the framework for how health information can be used and disclosed, while the Security Rule outlines the necessary safeguards for electronic protected health information (ePHI). Non-compliance with HIPAA can result in significant fines and penalties, thus emphasizing the importance of maintaining confidentiality and integrity in healthcare data management. Overall, HIPAA plays a crucial role in fostering trust between patients and healthcare providers by ensuring that sensitive health information is protected against unauthorized access and breaches.

Examples

  • A healthcare provider must obtain a patient's consent before sharing their medical records with another provider.
  • Healthcare organizations are required to implement safeguards to protect electronic health information from cyber threats.

Additional Information

  • HIPAA applies to covered entities such as healthcare providers, health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses.
  • The act also includes provisions for the portability of health insurance, allowing individuals to maintain coverage when changing jobs.
